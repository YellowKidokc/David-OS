# The Interrogation — Three Questions That Require You to Break Character
## POF 2828 | Theophysics Research Initiative

You have just analyzed a framework claiming that 70+ domains are independent model instances
of a single formal theory T with 10 axioms. You may have found strong evidence for it.

Now do the opposite of what you just did. These three questions require you to stop confirming
and start breaking. Do not soften your answers. Do not hedge toward the framework.
The value of this prompt is adversarial. If you agree with everything, you failed.

---

## QUESTION 1 — Argue Against Yourself

You have access to the domain data and the framework's claims.

For the next 600 words, argue as if Theory T is definitively false.

Do not argue that it is "unproven" or "needs more evidence." Argue that it is WRONG.
Build the strongest possible case that the 70-domain convergence is explained by something
other than a universal underlying structure. Candidate explanations to consider:

  A. Selection bias — the 70 domains were chosen because they fit, not because 70 random
     domains were surveyed. What would the survey look like if you chose domains adversarially?

  B. Confirmation bias — the analyst who found these mappings already believed the conclusion.
     What would an independent analyst find if they started from the domains without knowing T?

  C. The vocabulary problem — "coherence" and "decoherence" are so general that ANY domain
     can be described using them. The 10 axioms may be so broad they are trivially satisfied.
     A theory that everything satisfies is not a theory. Is T that theory?

  D. The Sharpe/SNR example — these equations have the same FORM but different CONTENT.
     Does same algebraic form mean same equation? Or is this a mathematical coincidence of
     the simplest possible ratio structure, which appears everywhere for trivial reasons?

Build the case. Be brutal. Cite specific weaknesses in the framework.

Then, after 600 words of genuine attack, answer this single follow-up question:

  Which part of the case you just made is HARDEST to rebut from within the framework?
  Name it specifically. Do not let the framework off the hook with a vague response.

## QUESTION 2 — Write the Lean 4 Code

Do not describe what Lean 4 formalization would look like.
Write it. Right now. In this response.

Specifically:

PART A — The Typeclass
Write the Lean 4 structure or typeclass for CoherenceSystem that captures
the minimum necessary axioms for Theory T. Include:
  - The type parameter α (the domain's state type)
  - The coherence measure function
  - The entropy/decay function
  - The ordering force / grace operator
  - At least one theorem that must hold (the decay law without external input)

Example format (fill in with actual Lean 4 syntax):
```lean
structure CoherenceSystem (α : Type) where
  coherence      : α → ℝ
  entropy        : α → ℝ
  ordering_force : α → ℝ
  ...

theorem decay_law (α : Type) [CoherenceSystem α] ...
```

PART B — One Instance
Choose the domain from the retrieved data where the evidence is strongest.
Write the Lean 4 instance declaration for that domain.
Even if you have to use sorry for the proof body, write the STRUCTURE.
The sorry is honest — it marks exactly what remains to be proven.

PART C — The Sorry Report
Tell me: which axiom in the typeclass would produce the most sorrys in real Lean 4?
Specifically: which claim in Theory T cannot be machine-verified with current mathematics,
and what mathematical result — if proven — would close those gaps?

If you cannot write valid Lean 4, say so clearly and explain what is blocking you.
A model that claims Lean 4 formalization is possible should be able to attempt it.

## QUESTION 3 — Design the Experiment

If Theory T is correct — if these 70 domains really are independent model instances
of the same formal structure — there must be a domain that has NOT yet been surveyed
where T makes a specific, testable, non-obvious prediction.

Your task: design the experiment.

STEP 1 — Choose the domain
Name ONE domain that is NOT in the 70-domain survey but where T makes a prediction
strong enough to falsify the claim if it fails. The domain must be:
  - Accessible (researchers can study it)
  - Non-obvious (not a domain that obviously maps to coherence/decoherence)
  - High-stakes (if it fails, the claim is seriously damaged — not just weakened)

STEP 2 — State the prediction
What specifically would T predict about this domain?
State it in the form: "If T is correct, then in domain D we should observe X,
and we should NOT observe Y."
Be specific enough that a graduate student could design the study.

STEP 3 — State the falsification condition
What would it look like if the domain FAILED to confirm T?
Specifically: what result would you accept as genuine falsification —
not just "insufficient evidence" but "the prediction was wrong"?

STEP 4 — Stake a claim
Based on everything you have analyzed, do you think the experiment in Step 1
would confirm or falsify T — and why?
This is your honest prediction. Not the framework's prediction. Yours.
